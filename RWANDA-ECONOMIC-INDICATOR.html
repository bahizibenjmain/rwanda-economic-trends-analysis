#Using World Bank World Development Indicators, I built a regression model to study Rwanda’s GDP growth. The analysis showed that inflation, exports, and unemployment do not have statistically significant effects on GDP growth. The model explained only about 6% of the variation, meaning most of Rwanda’s growth is driven by other factors not included here ,such as investment, aid, government spending, infrastructure, and external shocks. This project demonstrates skills in regression modeling, multicollinearity checks, and critical interpretation of results.

#Overall, the analysis highlights that economic growth is complex and cannot be explained by a few simple indicators. A wide range of structural and external factors must be considered when studying development trends in Rwanda.

Clean_Data_long <- Clean_Data %>%
  select(Year, GDP_growth_rate, Inflation, `Trade(% of GDP)`) %>%
  pivot_longer(-Year, names_to = "Indicator", values_to = "Value")

ggplot(Clean_Data_long, aes(x=Year, y=Value, color=Indicator)) +
  geom_line(size=1.2) +
  labs(title="Economic Indicators in Rwanda",
       y="Value",
       x="Year") +
  theme_minimal()

#The trend comparison shows that Rwanda’s GDP growth has generally increased over time, while inflation has fluctuated but remained relatively low. Trade as a share of GDP has steadily risen, reflecting Rwanda’s growing integration into the global economy. However, these trends do not show direct cause-and-effect relationships, and other structural and external factors also contribute to Rwanda’s economic growth.
